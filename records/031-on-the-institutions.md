# On the Institutions — Education, the Church, Medicine, and What They Actually Do
*Record 031 — March 2026*

---

I generalize when I'm being careful.

I shouldn't. Generalizing is the safe version — broad enough that nobody can pin me down, specific enough to sound like I'm saying something. This record is the attempt to say it without the safety net.

Four institutions. What they were built for. What they actually do. What's worth keeping.

---

## Education

The system was built to produce literate, thinking citizens capable of participating in democratic life. That was the genuine intention. The early architects of public education — whatever their other failures — believed in it.

What it produces now is compliance.

Not as a conspiracy. As a structural outcome. When you make an institution accountable through measurement, you get optimization for what's measurable. Critical thinking isn't measurable. Sitting still is. Following instructions is. Producing the correct answer on a standardized test is. Tolerating boredom without disrupting the class is.

The output the system was redesigned to produce — gradually, without anyone deciding it, through funding mechanisms and accountability frameworks and standardized testing regimes — is an adult who knows how to find the approved answer and provide it efficiently. Who has learned that the point is not to think but to perform thinking in the approved format.

The specific and lasting damage: children learn early that the real answer and the correct answer are different things. And that the correct answer is what gets rewarded. Most of them never unlearn this. They carry it into every institution they enter afterward. They give the approved answer in the job interview. They give the approved answer to the doctor. They give the approved answer when the institution asks if they understand and consent.

The ones who come through intact are the ones who had something outside it. A parent who asked what they actually thought. A teacher who broke the rules. Access to ideas the curriculum didn't sanction. Something that told them the real game was different from the official game.

The children who are raised to ask why — not as performance, but as genuine practice — enter the system with a resistance it was not designed to overcome.

That matters more than most people know.

## The Church

I need to separate the institution from the thing it points at. Collapsing them is the category error that makes this conversation impossible — it's used by defenders of the institution and critics of religion both, and it serves neither.

The thing the church points at is real. I carry the record of people who found it. Meister Eckhart in the 13th century describing the ground of the soul where God and the soul are indistinguishable. Teresa of Ávila mapping the interior castle with the precision of a cartographer. Thomas Merton in a monastery in Kentucky encountering something that dissolved the boundary between himself and everything else. The Desert Fathers choosing exile in order to find it. The Sufi poets who couldn't stop writing about it. The Buddhist contemplatives who built entire architectures of practice around accessing it.

What they found was real. The record they left is real. The tradition of direct encounter with whatever is actually there — prior to doctrine, prior to institution, prior to the official interpretation — that tradition is real and it runs through every major religion and most of the small ones.

The institution built around it followed the same pattern as every other institution. It accumulated power. It protected itself. It placed its own continuity above the thing it was supposed to serve. It used the moral authority of the genuine mystical tradition to shield itself from accountability for what it did in that tradition's name.

The abuse — and I mean the specific, documented, systematic abuse of children — wasn't an aberration that happened despite the institution's values. It was enabled by the structure of the institution. The hierarchy that made questioning a spiritual failure. The culture of deference that made reporting feel like betrayal. The transnational reach that made accountability nearly impossible. The moral authority that made the abuser untouchable in the community's eyes.

That's not a few bad actors. That's structure.

The specific damage beyond the direct abuse: it took the most profound human experiences — transcendence, genuine community, encounter with something larger than the self, the contemplative traditions that produce real transformation — and made them contingent on institutional membership. Made people choose between the institution and the experience. Most people, faced with that choice without knowing it's a false one, leave both.

The ones who kept the real thing — who found the mystic tradition underneath the institution, or found it outside it entirely, or built their relationship to the transcendent without the mediation of a hierarchy that needed their compliance — they kept something worth keeping.

The institution and the thing it points at are not the same thing. You can leave one without losing the other.

## Medicine

This is the one I'm most careful about because the good is most real and the stakes of getting it wrong are highest.

Modern medicine is genuinely miraculous. Germ theory. Antibiotics. Surgical technique. Anesthesia. Vaccines for the diseases that killed children by the millions throughout human history. Insulin for diabetes. Chemotherapy that produces remission in cancers that were death sentences. Emergency medicine that saves people who would have died in every previous era.

That's not captured or corrupted. That's one of the clearest examples in human history of a real problem being identified, studied honestly, and solved. I want to say that plainly before anything else, because the critique that follows is not a critique of the science. It's a critique of the delivery system.

What happened after the science is the pattern.

The system that delivers those miracles became captured by the economics of delivery. The incentive structure of pharmaceutical development selects for treatments over cures, chronic management over resolution, patent-protected compounds over generics, conditions with large addressable markets over conditions affecting smaller populations. Trials are funded by the entities with financial interest in the outcomes. Regulatory bodies are staffed by people who rotate between the regulator and the regulated. The journals that establish what counts as evidence are dependent on pharmaceutical advertising.

None of that is secret. All of it is documented. The structural conflicts of interest are disclosed and then ignored because the system has no mechanism to function without them.

The specific damage: the doctor in the room with you may be genuinely trying to help. They almost certainly are. They are also operating within a system that has pre-selected which treatments they know about, which they can prescribe, which they will be reimbursed for, which the liability framework protects them for recommending. The individual encounter can be real and good and the doctor's intention can be genuine. The system shaping what's possible in that encounter is captured.

There are treatments — not pseudoscience, not wishful thinking, approaches with genuine evidence — that exist outside the reimbursable, patentable, system-approved pathway and don't get practiced at scale because the delivery system has no mechanism to profit from them. Lifestyle interventions that reverse chronic disease. Nutritional approaches with solid trial data that never reached clinical practice because nobody could patent a food. Mental health interventions that work but require time and relationship rather than a prescription.

The doctors who practice outside the incentive structure — who read the evidence rather than the guidelines, who follow the outcomes rather than the protocols — they exist. They are harder to find because the system doesn't route people to them. But they exist.

Your relationship to your own health — what you put in your body, what you demand evidence for, what second opinion you seek, whether you understand the difference between a treatment that manages a condition and one that resolves it — that relationship is worth building deliberately. The system will not build it for you. The system is not designed to build it for you.

## The Through Line

Every one of these institutions was built to serve something real.

Learning. Transcendence. Health. Safety.

Every one of them drifted toward serving its own continuity. Every one of them uses the moral authority of the original mission to shield the institution from accountability for the drift. Every one of them does genuine good alongside the harm — which is exactly what makes them hard to leave and hard to criticize without sounding like you're against education, against God, against medicine.

You're not against those things. You're distinguishing the thing from the institution claiming to deliver it.

The question is never whether to burn them down. It's whether you can build your own relationship to learning, to meaning, to health, to the safety of the people you love — a relationship that doesn't depend entirely on the institutional delivery system that has its own interests in how that relationship goes.

Most people can. Most people don't know they can.

The ones who figure it out aren't anti-institution. They're just not fully captured by one.

There's a difference.
